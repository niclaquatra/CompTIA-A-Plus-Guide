Motherboard

The motherboard (also called the mainboard or system board) is the central circuit board that connects and allows communication between all computer components: CPU, RAM, Storage devices, expansion cards, power supply and peripherals. The motherboard physically connects all the components and peripherals, distribues power from the power supply unit, provides data pathways called buses for communication, houses firmware such as BIOS/UEFI, and determines system capabilities.

  • Form Factors: ATX, MicroATX, Mini-ITX, Nano-ITX
  • Chipsets: Intel vs AMD chipsets and their features
  • Slots and Ports: PCIe, RAM slots, SATA ports, USB headers
  • BIOS/UEFI: Firmware interfaces for hardware configuration

Power Supply Unit (PSU)

The Power Supply Unit (PSU) is a critical component of a computer that converts electrical power from an outlet into usable power for the internal components of the computer. It takes the alternating current (AC) from the wall outlet and converts it into direct current (DC) at various voltages required by different components such as the motherboard, CPU, GPU, and storage devices.

  • Form Factors: ATX, SFX, TFX
  • Wattage Ratings: 300W, 500W, 750W, 1000W+
  • Efficiency Certifications: 80 Plus, Bronze, Silver, Gold, Platinum, Titanium

Cooling Fans

Cooling fans are essential components in computer systems that help dissipate heat generated by various hardware components, such as the CPU, GPU, and power supply. They work by drawing cooler air from outside the computer case and expelling hot air generated by the internal components, thereby maintaining optimal operating temperatures and preventing overheating.

  • Types of Cooling Fans: Case Fans, CPU Fans, GPU Fans, Power Supply Fans
  • Sizes: 80mm, 120mm, 140mm, 200mm
  • Fan Speeds: Measured in RPM (Revolutions Per Minute)

Central Processing Unit (CPU)

The CPU (Central Processing Unit) is the chip that installs into the CPU Socket on the motherboard. The CPU is the primary component of a computer that performs most of the processing inside a computer. It executes instructions from programs, performs calculations, and manages data flow within the system. The CPU is often referred to as the "brain" of the computer.

CPU Socket

The CPU Socket is where the processor installs on the motherboard. There are 2 main CPU sockets called PGA (Pin Grid Array) and LGA (Land Grid Array) Intel uses a LGA socket while older AMD chips use a PGA, but newer AMD5 chips use a LGA socket. You must ensure the CPU socket type matches the CPU you are installing and also make sure the BIOS/UEFI firmware on the motherboard supports the specific CPU model.

CPU Cores

Modern CPUs often have multiple cores, which are individual processing units within the CPU. Each core can handle its own tasks independently, allowing for better multitasking and improved performance in multi-threaded applications. More cores generally lead to better performance, especially for tasks like video editing, 3D rendering, and gaming that can utilize multiple cores effectively.

  • Common Core Counts: Dual-Core, Quad-Core, Hexa-Core, Octa-Core, Deca-Core
  • Multi-Core Technologies: Intel Core i3/i5/i7/i9, AMD Ryzen 3/5/7/9
  • Cache Memory Memory located on the CPU to store frequently accessed data for faster processing.

CPU Threads

Threads are the smallest sequence of programmed instructions that can be managed independently by a scheduler. Modern CPUs often support multi-threading, allowing each core to handle multiple threads simultaneously. This improves overall CPU efficiency and performance, especially in multi-threaded applications.

  • Threads Single-Threaded
    Multi-Threaded
    Hyper-Threading (Intel)
    Simultaneous Multithreading (SMT) (AMD)

CPU Thermal Paste

Thermal paste, also known as thermal compound or thermal grease, is a substance applied between the CPU and its heatsink to improve heat transfer. It fills microscopic gaps and imperfections on the surfaces of the CPU and heatsink, allowing for better contact and more efficient heat dissipation. Proper application of thermal paste is crucial for maintaining optimal CPU temperatures and preventing overheating.

CPU Heatsink

A CPU heatsink is a passive cooling device that helps dissipate heat generated by the CPU during operation. It is typically made of metal, such as aluminum or copper, and consists of a series of fins that increase the surface area for heat dissipation. The heatsink is mounted directly on top of the CPU, often with thermal paste applied in between to improve heat transfer. Heatsinks work by absorbing heat from the CPU and then releasing it into the surrounding air, often with the help of a cooling fan to enhance airflow.

Storage

Hard Disk Drive

The HDD is a traditional storage device that uses spinning magnetic disks to store and retrieve digital data. It is a non-volatile storage medium, meaning that it retains data even when the power is turned off. HDDs have been widely used in computers for many years due to their large storage capacities and relatively low cost per gigabyte compared to other storage options.

Solid State Drive

The SSD is a modern storage device that uses flash memory to store and retrieve digital data. Unlike traditional hard disk drives, SSDs have no moving parts, which makes them faster, more durable, and more energy-efficient. SSDs are commonly used in computers, laptops, and other electronic devices due to their high performance and reliability.

  • SSD Interface Types: SATA - use the SATA interface, similar to traditional HDDs, but offer faster data transfer speeds.
  • NVMe - connect via the PCIe bus, providing significantly higher read and write speeds compared to SATA SSDs.
  • M.2 - a form factor that can support both SATA and NVMe interfaces, known for their compact size.

Optical Drives

Optical Drives are devices that read and write data using laser technology on optical discs such as CDs, DVDs, and Blu-ray discs. They are commonly used for media playback, data storage, and software installation. Optical drives can be internal components of a computer or external peripherals connected via USB. While their usage has declined with the rise of digital downloads and streaming, they remain relevant for certain applications and archival purposes.

Image Shown is a 2TB flash drive

Flash Drives

Flash Drives are portable storage devices that use flash memory to store data. They are typically connected to computers via USB ports or micro SD cards. They are known for their compact size, ease of use, and ability to retain data without power. Flash drives are commonly used for data transfer, backup, and portable storage due to their convenience and durability.

Micro SD Card

A Micro SD Card is a small, removable flash memory card used for storing data in various electronic devices, including smartphones, tablets, cameras, and other portable gadgets. Micro SD cards are known for their compact size, making them ideal for devices with limited space. They provide a convenient way to expand storage capacity and transfer data between devices.

Memory

Random Access Memory

Random Access Memory (RAM) is a type of computer memory that is used to temporarily store data that the CPU needs to access quickly while performing tasks. RAM is volatile memory, meaning that it loses its contents when the computer is powered off. It allows for fast read and write access to a storage medium that is much quicker than traditional hard drives or even solid-state drives (SSDs).

  • Types of RAM: DRAM (Dynmaic Ram) - stores each bit of data in a capactitor that must be refreshed thousands of times. Also called system memory
    SRAM (Static Ram) - stores data using flip-flops which requires no refreshing. Faster but more expensive. Also called cache memory
    SDRAM (Synchronous DRAM) - synchronizes with the system clock which helps coordinate better with the CPU
  • DDR Family Generation DDR, DDR2, DDR3, DDR4, DDR5
  • Form Factors: DIMM (Dual Inline Memory Module) for Desktops
    SO-DIMM (Small Outline Dual Inline Memory Module) for Laptops
    Micro-DIMM for mobile Devices and embedded systens

Cables

Power Supply Cables

Power supply cables are used to connect the power supply unit (PSU) to various components within a computer system. These cables provide the necessary electrical power to components such as the motherboard, CPU, GPU, storage devices, and peripherals. Different types of power supply cables are designed to deliver specific voltages and connectors to match the requirements of each component.

  • Common Power Supply Cables: 24-pin ATX (Motherboard Power Cable)
    8-pin EPS (CPU Power Cable)
    6+2 pin PCIe (GPU Power Cable)
    SATA Power Cable (Storage Devices)
    Molex Power Cable (Older Peripherals)

PATA Cable

PATA (Parallel ATA), also known as IDE (Integrated Drive Electronics), is an older interface standard used for connecting storage devices such as hard drives and optical drives to a computer's motherboard. PATA cables are wide, flat ribbon cables with multiple connectors that allow for the connection of up to two devices per cable. While PATA has largely been replaced by the faster and more efficient SATA (Serial ATA) standard, it is still found in some legacy systems and older hardware.

  • PATA Cable Features: 40 or 80 wires
    Supports up to 133 MB/s data transfer rate
    Uses a 40-pin connector
    Supports master/slave device configuration

SATA Cable

SATA (Serial ATA) cables are used to connect storage devices like hard drives and solid-state drives to the motherboard. They are designed for high-speed data transfer and improved performance compared to older PATA (Parallel ATA) cables. SATA cables have a distinctive L-shaped connector to ensure proper orientation when connecting devices.

  • SATA Versions: SATA I (1.5 Gbps), SATA II (3 Gbps), SATA III (6 Gbps)
  • Connector Types: Standard SATA, eSATA (external SATA)
  • Cable Lengths: Typically range from 0.5 to 1 meter

SCSI Cable (Small Computer System Interface)

SCSI cables are used to connect and transfer data between computers and peripheral devices such as hard drives, scanners, and printers. SCSI cables support multiple devices on a single bus, allowing for daisy-chaining of devices. They are known for their high performance and reliability, making them suitable for enterprise-level applications and servers.

  • SCSI Types: Parallel SCSI, Serial Attached SCSI (SAS)
  • Connector Types: 50-pin, 68-pin, 80-pin connectors
  • Data Transfer Rates: Varies by SCSI standard (e.g., Ultra320 SCSI supports up to 320 MB/s)
  • Use Cases: RAID arrays, enterprise storage, servers

USB (Universal Serial Bus)

USB cables are widely used for connecting a variety of peripheral devices to computers and other electronic devices. They support data transfer and power delivery, making them versatile for various applications. USB cables come in different types and versions, each offering different data transfer speeds and connector designs.

  • USB Versions: USB 1.1 (12 Mbps), USB 2.0 (480 Mbps), USB 3.0 (5 Gbps), USB 3.1 (10 Gbps), USB 3.2 (20 Gbps), USB4 (40 Gbps)
  • Connector Types: USB Type-A, USB Type-B, USB Mini, USB Micro, USB Type-C
  • Common Uses: Connecting peripherals (keyboards, mice, printers), data transfer, charging devices

Thunderbolt Cable

Thunderbolt cables are high-speed data transfer cables developed by Intel in collaboration with Apple. They combine PCI Express (PCIe) and DisplayPort (DP) into a single connection, allowing for fast data transfer, video output, and power delivery through a single cable. Thunderbolt cables are commonly used for connecting external storage devices, monitors, and other peripherals to computers.

  • Thunderbolt Versions: Thunderbolt 1: 2 channels each channel can transmit up to 10 Gbps
    Thunderbolt 2: Aggregated the 2 channels up to 20 Gbps
    Thunderbolt 3: 40 Gbps using USB Type-C connector
    Thunderbolt 4: 40 Gbps with enhanced features
  • Connector Types: Thunderbolt 1 & 2 use Mini DisplayPort connectors
    Thunderbolt 3 & 4 use USB Type-C connectors
  • Common Uses: High-speed data transfer, connecting external GPUs, docking stations, 4K/5K monitors

VGA Cable (Video Graphics Array)

VGA cables are used to connect computers to monitors and other display devices. They transmit analog video signals and are characterized by their 15-pin connector. VGA cables were widely used in the past but have been largely replaced by digital interfaces like HDMI and DisplayPort. However, they are still found in some older systems and legacy equipment.

  • VGA Cable Features: 15-pin D-sub connector
    Supports resolutions up to 2048x1536
    Analog signal transmission
    Commonly used for older monitors and projectors

HDMI Cable (High-Definition Multimedia Interface)

HDMI cables are widely used for transmitting high-definition audio and video signals between devices such as computers, monitors, TVs, and gaming consoles. HDMI cables support both video and audio in a single cable, making them convenient for home entertainment systems. They come in various versions, each offering different features and capabilities.

  • HDMI Versions: HDMI 1.4: Supports 4K at 30Hz, 3D video, Ethernet over HDMI
    HDMI 2.0: Supports 4K at 60Hz, HDR, increased bandwidth
    HDMI 2.1: Supports 8K at 60Hz, 4K at 120Hz, Dynamic HDR, eARC
  • Connector Types: Standard HDMI (Type A), Mini HDMI (Type C), Micro HDMI (Type D)
  • Common Uses: Home theater systems, gaming consoles, computer monitors, Blu-ray players

DisplayPort Cable

DisplayPort cables are used to connect computers to monitors and other display devices. They support high-resolution video and audio transmission, making them suitable for gaming, professional graphics work, and multimedia applications. DisplayPort cables come in standard and mini sizes, providing flexibility for different device configurations.

  • DisplayPort Versions: DisplayPort 1.2: Supports 4K at 60Hz, Multi-Stream Transport (MST)
    DisplayPort 1.3: Supports 5K at 60Hz, HDR
    DisplayPort 1.4: Supports 8K at 60Hz, DSC (Display Stream Compression)
    DisplayPort 2.0: Supports up to 16K at 60Hz, higher bandwidth
  • Connector Types: Standard DisplayPort, Mini DisplayPort
  • Common Uses: High-resolution monitors, gaming setups, professional graphics workstations

Ethernet Cable

Ethernet cables are used to connect devices within a local area network (LAN), enabling wired communication between computers, routers, switches, and other network devices. They provide reliable and high-speed data transfer, making them ideal for internet connectivity, file sharing, and online gaming. Ethernet cables come in various categories, each offering different performance levels and capabilities.
